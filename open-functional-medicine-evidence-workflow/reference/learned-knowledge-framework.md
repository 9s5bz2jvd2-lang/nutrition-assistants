# Learned Knowledge Framework

> **Purpose.** A clean-room, human-readable conceptual map of what was learned
> from the old functional-medicine study corpus. It organizes facts, ideas,
> relationships, and methods by theme and inquiry — not by textbook chapter
> order. Every learned item is tagged so readers can see its epistemic status.

---

## 1  How to read the tags

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `textbook-claim` | A claim or framework that the source textbooks present as part of functional medicine. It is not independently verified here. |
| `unverified-general-fact` | A factual statement that is broadly plausible or commonly cited, but must be checked against an independent authority before being used as evidence. |
| `contested-hypothesis` | An idea that is explicitly debated in mainstream medicine or lacks consistent evidence. |
| `high-risk-not-actionable` | Content that could lead to unsafe self-treatment; must not be turned into action, dosage, or diagnosis. |
| `human-original-method` | A method asserted by Wang Runyuan as human-original: the Six-Eye lens and evidence-governance workflow. These are governance/completeness methods, not validated clinical tools. |
| `clean-room-product-method` | An original operational arrangement authored for this Skill from generic organizational techniques. It is not evidence of clinical validity or a license to reproduce a source framework. |

## 2  Core functional-medicine ideas as educational scaffolding

### 2.1  Definition and orientation

- **Functional medicine is presented as a cause-seeking, systems-oriented way of asking why dysfunction develops.** (`textbook-claim`)
  - The textbook contrast with conventional medicine is pedagogical, not a claim of superiority: it emphasizes function, lifestyle, patient participation, and longer timelines rather than acute disease management.
  - In this Skill, the contrast is used only to generate questions, not to rank medical systems.

- **Six principles** (`textbook-claim`)
  1. Biochemical individuality.
  2. Patient-centered care.
  3. Body-mind-spirit as an integrated whole.
  4. Network-like interconnection of physiological systems.
  5. Health primacy — absence of diagnosed disease is not the same as health.
  6. Building physiological reserve and resilience.

- **Two core questions and their six influencing factors** (`textbook-claim`)
  - The textbooks ask: why has this person lost function? and what factors maintain dysfunction?
  - Influencing factors include genetics, environment, diet, lifestyle, stress/psychosocial factors, and prior medical/toxin exposures.
  - These are organizing questions, not diagnostic criteria.

### 2.2  The antecedent / trigger / mediator model

- **Three categories of contributing factors** (`textbook-claim`)
  - **Antecedents** — pre-existing predispositions (genetics, early life, chronic diet, environment, trauma, medication history).
  - **Triggers** — events that appear to start or worsen a change (infection, injury, allergen, toxin, major stressor).
  - **Mediators** — ongoing biological or psychosocial processes that keep the change active (hormones, cytokines, oxidative stress, social determinants, beliefs/behaviors).
  - This is a *question organizer*, not a causal proof.

### 2.3  Seven physiological imbalance domains

The textbooks describe seven overlapping domains. Here they are framed as **question domains** rather than diagnostic labels.

1. **Digestion, absorption, and gut ecology** (`textbook-claim` + `contested-hypothesis`)
   - Questions: Is nutrient breakdown and absorption supported? Is the gut microbiota diverse and stable? Is intestinal permeability a relevant concern? Is immune tolerance in the gut balanced?
   - The "leaky gut" concept is a hypothesis; its diagnostic standards and causal role in specific diseases remain debated (`contested-hypothesis`).

2. **Immune balance and chronic inflammation** (`textbook-claim` + `unverified-general-fact`)
   - Questions: Are inflammatory mediators elevated? Is there evidence of immune dysregulation (e.g., Th1/Th2 bias in textbook framing)? Are infections, allergens, or autoimmunity part of the picture?
   - CRP, IL-6, TNF-α are real biomarkers, but their interpretation requires clinical context (`unverified-general-fact`).

3. **Detoxification and biotransformation** (`textbook-claim` + `contested-hypothesis`)
   - Questions: Are Phase I and Phase II liver pathways functionally balanced? Is exposure to environmental toxins documented? Is glutathione status or methylation capacity relevant?
   - "Detox" protocols, heavy-metal chelation, and sauna-based elimination are high-risk and not actionable here (`high-risk-not-actionable`).

4. **Hormone and neurotransmitter communication** (`textbook-claim` + `contested-hypothesis`)
   - Questions: How do HPA, HPT, and HPG axes interact in this scenario? Is stress/cortisol rhythm relevant? Are thyroid conversion or sex-hormone balance part of the question set?
   - "Adrenal fatigue" as a standalone diagnosis is contested (`contested-hypothesis`).

5. **Oxidative stress and mitochondrial function** (`textbook-claim` + `unverified-general-fact`)
   - Questions: Is there evidence of excess reactive oxygen species? Are antioxidant networks (glutathione, vitamin C/E, CoQ10, α-lipoic acid) relevant? Is mitochondrial energy production a reasonable line of inquiry?
   - Mechanistic plausibility does not prove clinical efficacy (`unverified-general-fact`).

6. **Circulatory transport and endothelial function** (`textbook-claim` + `unverified-general-fact`)
   - Questions: Is lipid particle quality (e.g., small dense LDL in textbook framing) or endothelial nitric-oxide function worth exploring? Are vascular inflammation or hemodynamic factors present?
   - Lipid-lowering supplements, high-dose niacin, etc., are not actionable (`high-risk-not-actionable`).

7. **Structural integrity** (`textbook-claim`)
   - Questions: Is cell-membrane composition, musculoskeletal alignment, or tissue repair capacity relevant?
   - This is the least emphasized domain in the distilled materials.

### 2.4  General visual organization aids

- **Matrix worksheet, timeline, and issue tree** (`clean-room-product-method`)
  - Matrix worksheet: place the person's reported story, antecedent/trigger/mediator questions, system domains, MES context, and lifestyle factors in one bounded view.
  - Timeline: place reported events in chronological order without inventing causality.
  - Issue tree: organize competing questions and missing evidence without treating the branches as diagnoses.
  - These are generic teaching and reasoning aids, not diagnostic instruments.

### 2.5  Intervention levels

The textbooks describe five levels of intervention (`textbook-claim`):

1. Whole-organism / body-mind-spirit.
2. Organ-system support.
3. Cellular metabolism.
4. Subcellular / mitochondrial.
5. Gene expression / epigenetic.

In this Skill, these levels are used only to **generate evidence questions** (e.g., "what is known about mitochondrial-targeted interventions for this condition?"), never to prescribe.

## 3  Cross-domain concepts that appear repeatedly

### 3.1  Gut–liver–immune–brain axis

- The textbooks repeatedly link intestinal permeability, liver detoxification burden, immune activation, and neurobehavioral symptoms (`textbook-claim` + `contested-hypothesis`).
- This is best treated as a **set of testable questions**: Is there evidence of intestinal inflammation? Is liver function testing abnormal? Is there a plausible immune mediator? Are neurobehavioral symptoms present?
- No causal chain should be asserted without independent evidence.

### 3.2  Stress, HPA axis, and metabolic consequences

- Chronic stress → cortisol dysregulation → sleep disruption → insulin resistance → weight gain/inflammation is a common textbook narrative (`textbook-claim`).
- Each arrow is a hypothesis that requires independent verification (`unverified-general-fact`).

### 3.3  Nutrient cofactors and biochemical pathways

- Examples that appear in the textbooks: magnesium in enzyme reactions, zinc in immunity, selenium in glutathione peroxidase, B-vitamins in methylation, CoQ10 in electron transport, NAC as glutathione precursor, omega-3 fatty acids in inflammation (`unverified-general-fact`).
- These facts describe biochemical roles, not clinical recommendations. Any claim that a nutrient treats a condition is `high-risk-not-actionable`.

### 3.4  Genomics and SNPs

- SNPs are described as modifiers of enzyme activity or nutrient-cofactor demand, not deterministic disease causes (`textbook-claim`).
- Examples: MTHFR C677T, APOE E4, ALDH2, PPARG (`unverified-general-fact`).
- SNP → symptom → supplement adjustment is a high-risk causal jump (`high-risk-not-actionable`).

## 4  What this framework does NOT do

- This learned framework alone does not diagnose; it supplies questions and evidence-debt flags to the separately governed diagnostic-support workflow.
- It does not treat, prescribe, dose, or claim that any textbook mechanism is clinically effective.
- It does not reproduce cases, formulas, supplement tables, or proprietary frameworks.
- It does not replace guideline-based medical care.
