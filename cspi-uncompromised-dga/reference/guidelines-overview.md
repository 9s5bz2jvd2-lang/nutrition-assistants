# Guidelines Overview — The Five Uncompromised Guidelines

## GUIDELINE 1. Follow a Healthy Dietary Pattern at Every Life Stage

**Core message:** At every life stage — infancy, toddlerhood, childhood, adolescence, adulthood, pregnancy, lactation, and older adulthood — it is never too early or too late to eat healthfully.

### Life-stage decision tree

| Life stage | Key action |
|---|---|
| **0–6 months** | Exclusively feed human milk if possible; iron-fortified formula when human milk unavailable; supplemental vitamin D beginning soon after birth |
| **~6 months** | Introduce nutrient-dense complementary foods based on developmental readiness; include potentially allergenic foods alongside other foods; emphasize iron + zinc (especially for human-milk-fed infants) |
| **6–12 months** | Continue human milk through at least year 1; offer fruits, vegetables, grains of varying flavors/textures in child-appropriate portions |
| **< 2 years** | Avoid added sugars; limit foods and beverages high in sodium |
| **12 months – older adulthood** | Follow a healthy, enjoyable dietary pattern to meet nutrient needs, achieve healthy body weight, and reduce chronic disease risk |

### Infancy checklist
- [ ] Human milk exclusive for ~6 months if possible
- [ ] Iron-fortified formula if human milk unavailable
- [ ] Supplemental vitamin D from soon after birth
- [ ] At ~6 months: introduce complementary foods based on developmental readiness
- [ ] Introduce potentially allergenic foods alongside other complementary foods
- [ ] Emphasize iron-rich and zinc-rich foods for human-milk-fed infants
- [ ] Vary flavors and textures to promote acceptance
- [ ] No added sugars for children < 2 years
- [ ] Limit high-sodium foods and beverages for children < 2 years

## GUIDELINE 2. Customize and Enjoy Nutrient-Dense Food and Beverage Choices to Reflect Personal Preferences, Cultural Traditions, and Budgetary Considerations

**Core message:** Healthy eating is flexible. The Dietary Guidelines provides a flexible framework to be customized to individual needs, preferences, and the foodways of diverse cultures in the United States.

### Decision rules
- A healthy dietary pattern can benefit all individuals regardless of age, race, ethnicity, or health status
- Food should bring joy and comfort as well as nourishment
- Cultural practices and economic circumstances are valid constraints to work within, not obstacles to overcome
- **No one-size-fits-all**: the same healthy core pattern adapts to many foodways

---

## GUIDELINE 3. Focus on Meeting Food Group Needs with Nutrient-Dense Foods and Beverages, and Stay Within Calorie Limits

**Core message:** Nutritional needs should be met primarily from nutrient-dense foods and beverages. Nutrient-dense foods provide vitamins, minerals, and other health-promoting components and have no or little added sugars, saturated fat, and sodium.

### Core elements of a healthy dietary pattern

| Food group | Direction | Key subgroups |
|---|---|---|
| **Vegetables** | Increase variety | Dark green; red and orange; beans, peas, lentils; starchy; other |
| **Fruits** | Especially whole fruit | — |
| **Grains** | Mostly whole grains | **Increase** whole grains (whole wheat bread, brown rice, quinoa, corn tortillas, oatmeal); **decrease** refined grains |
| **Dairy & fortified soy alternatives** | Fat-free or low-fat (ages 2+) | Milk, yogurt, cheese; lactose-free; fortified soy beverages and yogurt |
| **Protein foods** | Shift sources | **Increase** beans, peas, lentils, nuts, seeds, soy products; **decrease** red and processed meat |
| **Oils** | Include | Vegetable oils higher in unsaturated fats; oils in seafood and nuts |

### Underconsumed nutrients to emphasize
- Vitamin D
- Calcium
- Potassium
- Dietary fiber

### Children-specific guidance
- Make healthy foods (fruits, vegetables) available at home
- Provide repeated exposures to new foods
- Model healthy eating behaviors
- Regular breakfast consumption is part of a healthy pattern (especially children and adolescents)

### Ultra-processed food note (CSPI)
> The DGAC concluded only limited evidence of greater health risks from ultra-processed foods due to inconsistent definitions and study methods. However, the collective recommendations suggest healthy dietary patterns should largely include whole, minimally processed foods, leaving limited room for highly processed foods high in calories, added sugar, sodium, and saturated fat.

## GUIDELINE 4. Limit Foods and Beverages Higher in Added Sugars, Saturated Fat, and Sodium, and Limit Alcoholic Beverages

**Core message:** Meeting food group recommendations — even with nutrient-dense choices — requires most of a person's daily calories. There isn't much room for extra added sugars, saturated fat, sodium, or alcoholic beverages.

### Quantitative limits (starting at age 2)

| Component | Limit | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Added sugars** | < 10% of calories/day | Avoid for children < 2 years |
| **Saturated fat** | < 10% of calories/day | Replace with unsaturated fat, especially polyunsaturated + monounsaturated from plant sources (nuts, whole grains, vegetables, vegetable oils) |
| **Sodium** | < 2,300 mg/day | Even less for children < 14 |
| **Alcoholic beverages** | See below | Adults of legal drinking age only |

### Alcohol guidance (2020 DGAC — not adopted by Departments)

| Recommendation | Source |
|---|---|
| Do not begin to drink or continue drinking because you think it will make you healthier | 2020 DGAC |
| At all levels of consumption, drinking less is generally better for health than drinking more | 2020 DGAC |
| Recommended limits: up to **1 drink/day for both women and men** | 2020 DGAC |

> ⚠️ The Departments did not adopt the 2020 DGAC's alcohol recommendations. The 2015–2020 DGA allowed up to 2 drinks/day for men. The 2025 DGAC deferred to two other committees whose work was either incomplete or never finalized. See `reference/alcohol-context.md`.

## GUIDELINE 5. Prioritize Foods and Healthy Habits That Support Sustainable Healthy Diets

**Source:** CSPI and Center for Biological Diversity addition (not from the 2025 DGAC, but science-based and consistent with the other Guidelines).

**Core message:** An environmentally sustainable dietary pattern is consistent with and supports the goals of the other Guidelines. Healthy diets can support planetary health.

### Sustainable diet practices

| Practice | Action | Evidence basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Prioritize plant-based proteins** | Protein should come predominantly from beans, peas, lentils, nuts, seeds, and soy products | Consistent with Guideline 3 protein shift |
| **Limit red and processed meat** | If you eat meat, limit intake; beef production has large environmental impact | Reduces GHG emissions, land use, water consumption |
| **Make tap water primary beverage** | If safe and appealing tap water is available | Decreases plastic bottle production and disposal |
| **Minimize food waste** | Plan meals, buy only what needed, serve reasonable portions, save leftovers, compost | USDA food waste prevention steps |
| **Choose unpackaged, whole foods** | Reduce single-use plastics when possible | Plastic pollution and chemical exposure concern |

### Sustainable healthy diet definition (FAO/WHO)
> Dietary patterns that promote all dimensions of individuals' health and wellbeing; have low environmental pressure and impact; are accessible, affordable, safe and equitable; and are culturally acceptable.

### Consistency with other Guidelines
- The healthy dietary pattern from Guideline 3 (higher in vegetables, fruits, legumes, nuts, whole grains, unsaturated fat; lower in red/processed meat, refined grains, sugar-sweetened foods/beverages) is already more sustainable than the average American diet
- Plant-based proteins are often part of traditional diets in many cultures
- Many nutrient-dense foods have lower environmental impact than energy-dense, nutrient-poor foods
