# Sustainable Diet — Guideline 5 Deep Dive

## Source and scope

Guideline 5 is **not** from the 2025 DGAC. It is a CSPI + Center for Biological Diversity addition, science-based and consistent with the other four Guidelines. The DGAC's Overarching Advice was solely intended to promote human health and prevent disease; Guideline 5 extends this to include environmental health.

For the full rationale, see: *A Model for Healthy and Sustainable Dietary Guidelines for Americans* (CSPI companion document).

## The sustainable healthy diet definition (FAO/WHO)

> Dietary patterns that promote all dimensions of individuals' health and wellbeing; have low environmental pressure and impact; are accessible, affordable, safe and equitable; and are culturally acceptable.

## Five sustainable diet practices

### 1. Prioritize plant-based proteins

| What | Why |
|---|---|
| Protein intake should come predominantly from beans, peas, lentils, nuts, seeds, and soy products | Consistent with Guideline 3's protein shift direction; plant-based proteins are often part of traditional diets; lower environmental impact |

### 2. Limit red and processed meat

| What | Why |
|---|---|
| If you eat meat, limit intake of red and processed meat | Beef production has large environmental impact; limiting red meat reduces GHG emissions, land use, and water consumption |

### 3. Make tap water your primary beverage

| What | Why |
|---|---|
| If safe and appealing tap water is available, choose it over bottled beverages | Decreases production and disposal of plastic bottles |

### 4. Minimize food waste

| What | Why |
|---|---|
| Plan meals before shopping; serve reasonable portions; save leftovers; compost | USDA food waste prevention steps; reduces environmental burden of wasted food |

### 5. Choose unpackaged, whole foods

| What | Why |
|---|---|
| Reduce single-use plastics when possible | Plastic pollution and exposure to potentially harmful chemicals in plastics |

## Consistency proof

The healthy dietary pattern from Guidelines 1–4 already supports sustainability:
- Higher in vegetables, fruits, legumes, nuts, whole grains, unsaturated fat → lower environmental impact
- Lower in red/processed meat, refined grains, sugar-sweetened foods/beverages → reduces GHG and resource use
- The "Eat Healthy Your Way" pattern is more sustainable than the average American diet by design

## Cross-references

- Guideline 3 → protein shift (increase plant-based, decrease red/processed meat) directly supports Guideline 5
- The DGAC's alcohol review (Guideline 4) does not address environmental impact of alcohol production
- Ultra-processed food note: collective recommendations suggest minimizing highly processed foods, which also supports sustainability (less packaging, less processing energy)
